<h2 class="wp-block-heading">1. <strong>Common Problems in Main Engine Exhaust Valves</strong></h2>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">1.1. <strong>Common Issues with Exhaust Valves</strong></h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>High-temperature corrosion of valve and seat</li>



<li>Reduced valve lift</li>



<li>Spring failure</li>



<li>Valve and valve seat erosion</li>



<li>Bent valve spindle</li>



<li>Localized burning on valve and seat</li>



<li>Broken valve rotator vanes</li>



<li>Carbon or molten salt deposits in exhaust passage</li>



<li>Poor cooling of valve cage</li>



<li>Reseating failure causing exhaust leakage</li>



<li>Valve banging on closure</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">1.2. <strong>Exhaust Valve Knocking Causes</strong></h4>



<p><strong>A. Oil System Deficiencies</strong></p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Air in the oil or air drawn into the pump suction side</li>



<li>Low oil supply pressure</li>



<li>High oil temperature reducing viscosity</li>
</ul>



<p><strong>B. Air System Deficiencies</strong></p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Incorrect spring air pressure</li>



<li>Faulty air chamber drain valve</li>



<li>Malfunctioning air chamber safety valve</li>
</ul>



<p><strong>C. High-Pressure Oil System Leaks</strong></p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Incorrect or faulty throttle screw adjustments</li>



<li>Leaking oil cylinder piston rings</li>



<li>Leaking cam-side oil cylinder safety valve</li>



<li>Damaged high-pressure pipe</li>



<li>Vent valve leakage at the top</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">2. <strong>Step-by-Step Procedure for Main Engine Exhaust Valve Overhaul</strong></h2>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>Place the exhaust valve on a wooden platform.</li>



<li>Loosen nuts to remove the safety strap and oil cylinder.</li>



<li>Discard O-rings from the air cylinder.</li>



<li>Remove flange screws from the top of the air piston.</li>



<li>Release air from the air cylinder.</li>



<li>Loosen the air piston from the conical locking ring.</li>



<li>Lift the valve housing from the spindle and clean the components.</li>



<li>Inspect wear and measure critical clearances using micrometers.</li>



<li>Check for valve face and seat damage, including burnout and scoring marks.</li>



<li>Measure spindle clearances at multiple points, ensuring wear is within acceptable limits.</li>



<li>Check the condition of thrust pads, actuators, and sealing surfaces.</li>



<li>Test the valve’s closing mechanism using compressed air and ensure no leaks.</li>



<li>Reassemble and verify operational integrity before reinstallation.</li>
</ol>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">3. <strong>Exhaust Valve Grinding for Marine Engines</strong></h3>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>For large 2-stroke engines, valve grinding should be performed carefully.</li>



<li>Inspect valve seats for pitting or corrosion, as severe damage requires replacement.</li>



<li>Ensure valve stems are straight.</li>



<li>Use manufacturer-recommended grinding tools.</li>



<li>Apply grinding paste to valve faces and seats until a continuous belt forms.</li>



<li>Ensure the belt is at least 1.5 mm wide and free from scratches.</li>



<li>After grinding, clean thoroughly and apply motor oil on the valve stems before reassembly.</li>



<li>Avoid repeated grinding without control to prevent thinning of the valve lid, which can compromise the valve’s strength and lead to failure.</li>
</ol>
