<p>The lubrication system of an engine supplies lubricating oil to the various moving parts of the engine. </p>



<p>Its main function is to enable the formation of a film of oil between the moving parts, which reduces friction and wear. The lubricating oil is also used as a cleaner and in some engines as a coolant.</p>



<p><strong>MAN B&amp;W crosshead lubrication:</strong></p>



<ul>
<li>The lower half of the bearing housing is formed by the top end of the connecting rod. It supports the crosshead pin over its entire length and the piston rod is bolted to the top half of the crosshead pin through a cut-in bearing top half. Oil supply to the crosshead is via a telescopic pipe from the main L.O. supply at a pressure of about 2.5 bar.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Main engine lubricating oil system</strong> – This system supplies lubricating oil to the engine bearings, and cooling oil to the pistons. Lubricating oil is pumped from the ME LO Circulating Tank, placed in the double bottom beneath the engine, using the ME LO Pump, to the ME LO Cooler, a thermostatic valve, and through a full-flow filter, to the engine, where it is distributed to the various branch pipes. Pumps and fine filters are arranged in duplicate, with one as a standby. </p>



<p>From the engine, the oil collects in the oil pan, from where it is drained to the ME LO Circulating Tank for reuse. A centrifuge is arranged for cleaning the lubricating oil in the system and the clean oil can be provided from a storage tank.</p>



<p><strong>Lubricating oil system</strong>: Lubricating oil for an engine is stored in the bottom of the crankcase, known as the sump, or in a drain tank located beneath the engine. The oil is drawn from this tank through a strainer, one of a pair of pumps, into one of a pair of fine filters. It is then passed through a cooler before entering the engine and being distributed to the various branch pipes. </p>



<p>The branch pipe for a particular cylinder may feed the main bearing, for instance. Some of this oil will pass along a drilled passage in the crankshaft to the bottom end bearing and then up a drilled passage in the connecting rod to the gudgeon pin or crosshead bearing.</p>



<p>An alarm at the end of the distribution pipe ensures that adequate pressure is maintained by the pump. Pumps and fine filters are arranged in duplicate with one as standby. The fine filters will be arranged so that one can be cleaned while the other is operating. After use in the engine the lubricating oil drains back to the sump or drain tank for re-use. A level gauge gives a local read-out of the drain tank contents. A centrifuge is arranged for cleaning the lubricating oil in the system and clean oil can be provided from a storage tank.</p>



<p>The oil cooler is circulated by sea water, which is at a lower pressure than the oil. As a result, any leak in the cooler will mean a loss of oil and not contamination of the oil by sea water.</p>



<p>Where the engine has oil-cooled pistons they will be supplied from the lubricating oil system, possibly at a higher pressure produced by booster pumps, e.g. Sulzer RTA engine. An appropriate type of lubricating oil must be used for oil-lubricated pistons to avoid carbon deposits on the hotter parts of the system.</p>



<p><strong>Cylinder lubrication</strong></p>



<p>Cylinder oil is pumped from Cylinder Oil Storage Tank to the Cylinder Oil Service Tank, placed min. 3000mm above the cylinder lubricators. The cylinder lubricators are mounted on the roller guide housing, and are interconnected with drive shafts. Each cylinder liner has a number of lubricating orifices, through which the cylinder oil is introduced into the cylinders via non-return valves.</p>



<p>Large slow-speed diesel engines are provided with a separate lubrication system for the cylinder liners. Oil is injected between the liner and the piston by mechanical lubricators which supply their cylinder, A special type of oil is used which is not recovered. As well as lubricating, it assists in forming a gas seal and contains additives that clean the cylinder liner.</p>



<p><strong>Lubricating Oil Sump Level</strong></p>



<p>The level of lubricating oil indicated in the sump when the main engine is running must be sufficient to prevent vortexing and ingress of air which can lead to bearing damage.</p>



<p>The sump level is to be according to the manufacturer/shipbuilder's instructions. The ‘Sump Quantity’ is always maintained at the same safe operating level and is given in liters. The figures must be mathematically steady and correct from month-to-month, taking into account consumption, losses, and refills and reported.</p>



<p>The ‘Sump Quantity’ is calculated with the engine stopped, but the lubricating oil pump is in operation, thus keeping the system oil in circulation.</p>



<p>Sufficient reserve quantities of lubricating oil must always be held, i.e. to fill the main sump and sufficient quantities of other lubes must be held to cover the intended voyage plus 20%. Lubricating oils are a major expenditure item, therefore, all purchasing must be pre-planned with the aim of buying the maximum amounts from the cheapest supply sources which are primarily the US, Europe, and Singapore. Lub oil requisitions should be sent to the office at least 10 days before the intended port of purchase and indicate if the vessel requires supply in bulk or in drums.</p>



<p><strong>Pre-Lubrication Pumps</strong></p>



<p>They provide an essential part of the lubrication system on many types of engines in particular auxiliary engines with engine-driven lubricating oil pumps.</p>



<p>They supply oil to the bearings before start-up limit the length of time that boundary lubrication exists, and shorten the time when hydrodynamic lubrication commences. They must be maintained and operated per the manufacturers’ instructions.</p>



<p>The crosshead on a slow-speed 2-stroke engine is difficult to lubricate effectively. The load is continually downward and because the con rod swings about the pin, changing direction each stroke, true hydrodynamic lubrication cannot take place. Instead, the lubrication starts as boundary lubrication, and as the rubbing speed increases, a hydrodynamic film is built up and as the rubbing speed decreases, the lubrication becomes boundary once again.</p>
